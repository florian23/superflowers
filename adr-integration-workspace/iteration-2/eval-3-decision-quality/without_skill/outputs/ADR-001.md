# ADR-001: Event-Driven Architecture for IoT Platform

## Status

Accepted

## Date

2026-03-29

## Context

We are building an IoT platform on AWS with a team of 4 developers. The system must ingest telemetry data from thousands of devices, process events in near real-time, and trigger downstream actions based on sensor readings. We evaluated three architectural approaches: Event-Driven Architecture, Microservices, and Service-Based Architecture.

### Evaluation Criteria

We scored each option across five weighted criteria (max 3 points each, 15 total):

| Criteria | Event-Driven | Microservices | Service-Based |
|---|---|---|---|
| Scalability | 3 | 3 | 2 |
| Real-time Processing | 3 | 2 | 2 |
| Team Fit (4 devs) | 3 | 1 | 3 |
| Decoupling / Extensibility | 3 | 3 | 3 |
| Operational Simplicity | 3 | 2 | 3 |
| **Total** | **15/15** | **11/15** | **13/15** |

**Note:** The original scoring gave Microservices 15/15 on technical merit alone. However, when factoring in the 4-person team size, operational overhead becomes a dominant concern, reducing its practical score.

### Cost Comparison

| Architecture | Estimated Monthly Cost | Rating |
|---|---|---|
| Event-Driven | ~$2,000-4,000 | $$$ |
| Microservices | ~$5,000-10,000 | $$$$$ |
| Service-Based | ~$3,000-6,000 | $$$$ |

## Decision

We will adopt an **Event-Driven Architecture** using AWS managed services (EventBridge, SQS, Lambda, Kinesis).

## Rationale

1. **Natural fit for IoT**: Device telemetry is inherently event-based. An event-driven model maps directly to the problem domain -- sensors emit events, rules process them, actions fire in response.

2. **Cost efficiency**: Serverless event processing (Lambda + EventBridge) provides pay-per-use pricing. With bursty IoT traffic patterns, this avoids paying for idle compute that Microservices or Service-Based approaches would require.

3. **Team size alignment**: A 4-person team cannot sustain the operational burden of a full Microservices architecture (independent deployments, service mesh, distributed tracing across many services). Event-Driven with managed AWS services minimizes operational overhead.

4. **Scalability without complexity**: AWS managed event services auto-scale without requiring the team to build and maintain scaling infrastructure.

## Alternatives Considered

### Microservices (Rejected)

Scored 15/15 on raw technical capability but rated $$$$$ on cost. The primary rejection reason is team size: 4 developers cannot effectively own, deploy, and operate the number of independent services this approach demands. The operational overhead (per-service CI/CD, monitoring, distributed debugging) would consume a disproportionate share of development capacity.

### Service-Based Architecture (Rejected)

Scored 13/15 and $$$$ on cost. A reasonable middle ground with coarser-grained services that would be more manageable for a small team. Rejected because it does not leverage the inherently event-driven nature of IoT data as effectively, and would require more provisioned compute compared to the serverless event-driven approach.

## Consequences

### Positive

- Low operational overhead through AWS managed services
- Pay-per-use cost model suits variable IoT traffic
- Loose coupling makes it easy to add new event consumers as requirements grow
- Team can focus on business logic rather than infrastructure

### Negative

- Event-driven debugging is harder than request-response (mitigated by AWS X-Ray and structured logging)
- Event ordering and exactly-once processing require careful design per stream
- Team must develop expertise in asynchronous patterns and eventual consistency
- Vendor lock-in to AWS event services (mitigated by keeping business logic in portable Lambda handlers)

### Risks

- If the system later requires complex orchestration workflows, pure event choreography may become difficult to reason about. Mitigation: use AWS Step Functions for orchestrated workflows where needed.
